Coaching Method

The Heart Rate System

Every training decision is anchored to your physiology — not pace, not ego, not peer pressure.

Zone-First Training

Your heart rate zones — not your watch's pace arrow — dictate every session. Z2 builds the aerobic engine. Z4 sharpens the blade. We train in the right zone at the right time.

HRV Readiness

Your body tells you whether to push or pull back — if you're listening. HRV data guides daily training decisions: green means go hard, red means recover. Never guess.

80 / 20 Principle

80% of weekly mileage in comfortable Z1–Z2. 20% quality in Z4–Z5. This polarised approach builds an aerobic base that lets you race hard without burning out.

Progressive Overload

10% rule, always. No week spikes volume beyond what your body can absorb. Load builds in waves — 3 weeks up, 1 week recovery. We race strong because we build smart.

Purpose-Driven Running

Running with a cause amplifies resilience. Whether it's your first 5K, a sub-4 marathon, or completing 100km, we run with intention — and we explain the why behind every session.

Beginner to Ultra

First run or 100th race — every runner gets a personalised entry point. The coach who once sat on a couch knows exactly how to meet you where you are and take you further.

Coach's Playbook

Tips & Tricks

Evidence-based, field-tested. The stuff that actually moves the needle.

Race Day
01

Slow Down to Speed Up

Most runners run too fast on easy days and too slow on hard days. If your easy run HR is above 75% max, you're in the grey zone — slow down. Aerobic base is built in Z2, not Z3.

02

Know Your Zones First

Without HR zones, every training plan is a guess. Calculate your max HR, find your zones, and wear that monitor. A $30 chest strap is the best training investment you'll make.

03

Check HRV Before Deciding

Is today a hard day or an easy day? Don't guess. Your HRV reading answers that. More than 10% below baseline? Rest. Above baseline? You're green to push. Trust the data.

04

Respect the 10% Rule

Never increase weekly mileage by more than 10% over the previous week. Injuries don't come from running hard — they come from running too much too soon. Build like a skyscraper.

05

HR Drift = Aerobic Fitness Signal

On a long easy run, if your HR creeps up 10%+ while pace stays constant — your aerobic base needs work. More Z2 miles fix this. It's called HR decoupling and it's very fixable.

06

Every 4th Week: Deload

Train 3 weeks, recover 1. Drop volume 20–30% on the 4th week while maintaining some intensity. This is when fitness gains are actually locked in — not during the hard weeks.

07

Start Slower Than You Think

For any race 10K and beyond: start 10–15 seconds per km slower than target pace. The field will surge. Let them. You'll catch most of them in the second half. Negative split wins.

08

Never Try New Gear on Race Day

Shoes, socks, gels, drinks — all must be tested in training. Race day is not the day to experiment. Your gear should be as familiar as your running form by the time you toe the line.

09

Sleep Is Training

8 hours of sleep does more for your running than an extra 5km easy jog. HRV tanks on poor sleep. Adaptation happens in recovery. Protect sleep like you protect your long run.
